## Parts → commerce assembly

None of the components above were built for their own sake, this is how they actually stack into the working commerce mockup, all on the Bootstrap 5.3 theme layer from [data-bs-theme="afj-dark"] . Nothing here is a new component; every piece below is one of the primitives already documented on this page, reused.

This is the point of documenting parts before assemblies: the cart drawer isn't a bespoke build, it's the card component's border pattern plus a Bootstrap offcanvas -style transform, themed by tokens that are already proven on the button and badge sections above. Anyone extending the commerce flow reaches for these same six primitives rather than inventing a seventh.

## Wine/Gold vs Scrollytelling

Two design systems living in one codebase only works if they're not actually competing for the same job. They aren't, each one owns a different moment in the visitor's relationship with AFJ Cardiff, and the palette and typography choices follow directly from that.

How they complement rather than compete. They never render in the same moment. Wine/Gold is calm on purpose because it shows up once someone already trusts the brand and wants to complete a task, booking, checkout, browsing classes. Scrollytelling is loud on purpose because it shows up before that trust exists, when the job is to earn attention and explain the culture behind the brand, not process a transaction. A visitor who lands on the scrollytelling teaser and later becomes a paying member should feel like they moved from "being told a story" to "being trusted to just get on with it", that shift in register is the two palettes doing their job.

Put simply: if a screen is asking someone to do something, it should look like Wine/Gold. If a screen is asking someone to feel something, it should look like Scrollytelling. Neither palette is "the real brand" and the other a variant, they're two tools built for two different jobs the same organisation genuinely has.

**Gherkin, what Wine/Gold serves**

```text
# Wine/Gold: transactional, operational, trust-first surfaces
Feature: Booking and commerce
  Scenario: Returning member books a weekly class
    Given a visitor already trusts the brand and knows what they want
    When they open the class list
    Then calm Montserrat type and a quiet wine/gold palette
      let the booking action be the only thing competing for attention

  Scenario: Customer completes a merch checkout
    Given a shopper has added items to the basket
    When they reach the checkout modal
    Then the interface stays legible and low-drama on purpose
      so nothing about the visual design creates hesitation at the point of payment
```

**Gherkin, what Scrollytelling serves**

```text
# Scrollytelling: narrative, discovery, pre-trust surfaces
Feature: First impressions and cultural storytelling
  Scenario: Cold visitor lands from an Instagram bio link before launch
    Given the visitor has never heard of AFJ Cardiff
    When they land on the scrollytelling page
    Then the gradient headline and grid overlay signal something
      worth paying attention to, before any commerce is even shown

  Scenario: Visitor needs to understand what Azonto actually looks like
    Given text alone cannot convey a dance movement
    When the visitor scrolls through the pinned story cards
    Then the cinematic red/orange system and Space Grotesk headlines
      carry the emotional weight text alone could not
```
